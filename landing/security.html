Security

Zero-knowledge.
Zero compromise.

Morrigan was designed from the ground up with one principle: we should never be able to read your data, even if we wanted to. Here's exactly how that works.

Architecture

How your vault is protected.

Client-Side Only

All encryption happens in your browser before data leaves your device. We receive only ciphertext.

Zero Server Knowledge

Our servers store your encrypted vault, but hold no keys. A breach of our servers reveals nothing readable.

Open for Audit

Every encryption implementation is open-source. Security researchers can and should verify our claims.

Cryptography

The cryptographic stack, explained.

XChaCha20-Poly1305
Primary symmetric cipher for vault content. 256-bit key, 192-bit nonce (vs AES-GCM's 96-bit — dramatically reduces nonce collision risk). Poly1305 provides authentication: if the ciphertext is tampered with, decryption fails. Audited in libsodium and the Signal Protocol.
Argon2id Key Derivation
Your passphrase is never used directly. Argon2id (winner of the Password Hashing Competition) derives the encryption key. Memory-hard: attackers need gigabytes of RAM per attempt — defeating GPU and ASIC attacks. Parameters: 64 MB memory, 3 iterations, 4 parallelism.
Shamir's Secret Sharing
The vault key is split into N shares using Shamir's scheme. Any M shares reconstruct the key (M < N). Example: 3-of-5 shares — you hold 2, two trusted contacts hold 1 each, Morrigan holds 0. No single party can access your vault.
OpenTimestamps (Bitcoin Anchoring)
We compute a SHA-256 hash of your vault state and submit it to OpenTimestamps. The hash is aggregated into a Merkle tree and anchored to the Bitcoin blockchain. This proves your vault existed at a specific time without revealing any content. You receive a .ots proof receipt you can verify independently.
Argon2id vs bcrypt vs scrypt
Why Argon2id? bcrypt is memory-light (attackable with cheap hardware). scrypt has parameter interaction issues. Argon2id won the Password Hashing Competition and is the current NIST recommendation. It combines resistance to time-memory tradeoff attacks (Argon2i) and GPU attacks (Argon2d).

Blockchain

Bitcoin as your notary.

We don't store a document on-chain. We store a fingerprint — a SHA-256 hash — that proves your vault existed in a specific state at a specific time.

01

Hash

Your vault content is hashed with SHA-256. The hash is a deterministic fingerprint — change one byte, the hash changes completely.

02

Merkle Tree

OpenTimestamps aggregates many hashes into a Merkle tree. This allows a single Bitcoin transaction to prove thousands of timestamps.

03

Bitcoin Anchor

The Merkle root is embedded in a Bitcoin transaction. This is confirmed in a Bitcoin block, creating a permanent, immutable record.

04

Proof Receipt

You receive a .ots file — an OpenTimestamps proof. Verify it independently using any OpenTimestamps client, with no dependence on Morrigan.

Post-Quantum Cryptography

She shifts form. She is never trapped in one state.

The Morrígan is the goddess of transformation. So too must encryption evolve. Classical cryptography — including RSA and elliptic curves — is vulnerable to Shor's algorithm on future quantum computers. We are preparing.

Roadmap v0.5.0

Kyber-1024

Key Encapsulation Mechanism. NIST PQC standard. Lattice-based. Replaces ECDH for key exchange.

NIST FIPS 203 (ML-KEM)
Roadmap v0.5.0

Roadmap v0.6.0

SPHINCS+

Stateless Hash-based Signatures. Conservative, hash-based. No lattice assumptions. Maximum conservatism.

Roadmap v0.6.0

HQC

Backup KEM. Code-based cryptography. NIST alternate candidate. Hedge against lattice vulnerabilities.
